Before the spreadsheets, there was a paintbrush.

The full story — how a painter became an investment advisor, why I also design and build brands, and what AI model evaluation has to do with financial modeling.

I started as a painter. Long before I built my first financial model, I was thinking about composition — why one arrangement of elements holds attention and another doesn't, what makes something worth looking at twice. That instinct never left. It found a second subject.

I studied Finance at Misr International University in Cairo, graduating in 2024 with a BBA. My thesis examined the effect of institutional quality on innovation — an early signal of the analytical framework I was building: how structural context shapes what's actually possible, in economies and in companies. Outside the classroom, I was building the kind of track record that doesn't appear on a transcript: leading AIESEC teams, competing in Model United Nations debates, organizing TEDx events. Rooms and arguments, not just spreadsheets.

The first serious public test of analytical judgment came in 2024 as a finalist at the CFA Society Egypt Research Challenge — competing against teams from the top five universities in Egypt. My team was recognized for outstanding investment research. A year later, I returned to the same competition as a judge and mentor, evaluating the analytical rigor of the next generation of candidates. I'm now a CFA Society Egypt Ambassador and active Level I candidate.

In parallel, I've been doing something most financial analysts don't: training and evaluating frontier AI models — GPT and Gemini — through Outlier, across many projects. The discipline is the same one financial modeling demands: identify the assumptions, find the inconsistency, fix the chain of reasoning. I've spent serious time inside how these models reason, and it changes how I think about structured judgment under uncertainty.

On the practitioner side, I've now worked on 22+ engagements across 15+ sectors — real estate, renewable energy, healthcare, hospitality, technology, logistics, manufacturing, fintech, FMCG, education, automotive, security services, catering, and restaurants. Egypt and Saudi Arabia are the two primary markets, but I've also modeled deals in the UAE and the US.

My current advisory practice spans two concurrent roles — one based in Cairo handling large-scale feasibility, due diligence, and sell-side mandates, and one covering the Gulf market remotely with Arabic-language financing proposals and institutional investor materials. I was promoted twice within twelve months in the primary role: from Financial Analyst to Investment Analyst to Senior Investment Advisor.

Earlier, I built the conventional foundation properly: internships at CIB Egypt and Banque du Caire in 2023, and the Financial Modeling & Equity Valuation workshop run by Al Ahly Pharos Securities Brokerage. Before the finance track, I founded Riptide, an early entrepreneurial venture — a useful lesson in what it actually takes to build something from scratch, even when it doesn't become a permanent institution.

The combination — finance, design, and now AI evaluation — isn't a pivot from something else. It's the same eye, pointed at three different surfaces. A DCF model and a brand identity system are structurally the same kind of object: assumptions in, structured logic, a defensible output.

Three principles

What guides the work.

01
Find the assumption before the counterparty does
Every model is only as strong as its weakest assumption. The job isn't to find a number — it's to find the number that holds up when someone on the other side of the table is actively trying to break it.
02
Analysis that can't be communicated isn't finished
Work that never reaches a decision-maker in a form they'll actually read and trust doesn't exist yet. The deck isn't decoration around the model — it's the model's job application to the room.
03
The sectors transfer. The rigor doesn't
Moving from real estate to healthcare to manufacturing requires learning new conventions. It doesn't require lowering the standard of analysis. I've done it fifteen times. The discipline is the same. The sector is just vocabulary.
