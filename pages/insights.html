JUN 2026
Investment Memos · IC Process

Why most IC memos lead with the number they want you to believe

The fastest way to lose a room of experienced investors is to show them only the best-case scenario. Most investment committee memos I've reviewed — including early drafts of my own — lead with the headline IRR or the NPV that makes the deal look most attractive, and bury the conservative-case numbers in an appendix that nobody opens before the meeting.

On a greenfield catering investment in Saudi Arabia, the scenario range I built ran from a 7-month payback at 67.9% IRR in the upside case to a substantially longer payback at a more moderate return in the conservative case. The temptation — always — is to lead with the number that makes someone want to invest. The document that actually earns trust leads with the case that's hardest to defend, then shows why the upside is plausible on top of it, not instead of it.

A committee that feels it's being sold the best case stops trusting the other numbers in the document. The skepticism is contagious and it spreads fast — once a reader suspects the memo was assembled to support a conclusion rather than to reach one, everything in it becomes suspect. One that sees the downside modeled with the same seriousness as the upside reads the whole document differently. It reads it as analysis rather than advocacy.

The practical implication: lead with the scenario that would kill the deal if it happened. If the answer to "what has to go wrong for this not to work?" is answered clearly and early, the rest of the memo gets read as credible.

Due Diligence · Document Review

A number that doesn't trace back to a source isn't a number yet

On a sell-side due diligence engagement for a Saudi industrial manufacturer, the company's pitch materials contained two different valuation figures across two documents. Neither was wrong on its own terms — they were calculated using different methodologies and different assumption sets. But they'd never been reconciled, and nobody had written down which one the company actually stood behind.

This is more common than it should be, and it's almost never intentional. Models get updated, decks don't. A consultant updates an assumption in a spreadsheet in November; the slide built in September uses the old number; both documents go to investors in December. The disconnect isn't fraudulent — it's the ordinary entropy of complex projects with multiple contributors and no single source of truth.

The fix isn't more sophisticated modeling. It's a simple discipline: every number in any client-facing document should trace back to a single, identified source — a specific cell in a specific file, with a specific version date. Anyone on the team should be able to answer "where does this come from?" for any figure in the deck, immediately, without having to look it up. If they can't, the number isn't ready.

The earlier in the process you check this, the cheaper it is to fix. Catching it in internal review costs nothing. Catching it when a counterparty's analyst asks about it in a due diligence call costs a lot — not in money, but in credibility, which is harder to rebuild than any financial model.

MAY 2026
Turnarounds · Financial Diagnosis

Diagnose before you prescribe: why the working assumption is usually wrong

A Saudi trading and retail company had lost 59% of its equity value across seven years of audited financials. The explanation offered internally, and repeated externally, was demand: the consumer environment had weakened, revenue growth had slowed, the business was facing structural headwinds that it couldn't control.

This was wrong. Not fabricated — the people saying it genuinely believed it. But wrong in a way that mattered enormously for what to do next. When I decomposed the P&L line by line across all seven years without starting from any conclusion, the picture was different: gross margins were stable. Revenue, while not growing fast, hadn't collapsed. The actual driver of the equity erosion was G&A cost inflation — overhead that had grown faster than the business, compounding quietly every year while the revenue line held.

The reason this matters: a demand problem gets fixed with pricing strategy, sales investment, and market repositioning. A cost problem gets fixed by cutting overhead. Apply the demand remedy to a cost problem and you spend money you don't have trying to grow revenue that isn't the issue, while the actual problem continues to compound underneath you.

Most turnaround situations I encounter have a working diagnosis already in the room when the conversation starts. The first job — before recommending anything — is to check whether the diagnosis is actually right. It often isn't. The people closest to a business are also the people most likely to have anchored on an explanation early and stopped looking for alternatives. The value of outside analysis is precisely that it starts without the anchor.

Cross-Border · Deal Structuring

The structure changes by market. The rigor required to analyze it doesn't

A US hotel LBO assessment — 56 keys, real monthly income statements, GP/LP waterfall with preferred-return tiers — was the first time I'd built a private equity real estate model in the US structural convention. The conventions are genuinely different from what I work with in Egypt and Saudi Arabia. The GP/LP split with a promote structure, the IRR hurdle tiers, the relationship between preferred return and catch-up — these aren't how transactions get structured in Cairo or Riyadh.

What was identical: the process of working backward from the income statement to figure out what the underlying business could actually support. The seasonal RevPAR patterns in the monthly data told you something about occupancy risk that no market comp report could replace. The exit-timing sensitivity showed something about waterfall dynamics that the headline IRR obscured. The same questions you'd ask about any financial model — where are the load-bearing assumptions, what happens if they're wrong, does the conclusion survive a stress test — turned out to be the right questions in Idaho as much as in Cairo.

Cross-border work teaches you to distinguish between conventions and disciplines. Conventions vary by market — the legal structure, the currency, the documentation norms. The discipline of working from source data, questioning the assumptions, and stress-testing the conclusion before any principal sees it doesn't vary. It's the same work in every language.

The practical implication for hiring: an analyst who has only ever modeled in one market has learned a set of conventions and might mistake them for the underlying discipline. An analyst who has modeled in four markets knows the difference.

APR 2026
Valuation · Balance Sheet Restatement

The gap between book value and what a company is worth is almost always in the assets

In a sell-side engagement for an Egyptian BPO operator, the pre-restatement book value understated the company's real position materially. The business had been carrying real property assets at historical cost for years — a common pattern in owner-managed Egyptian companies where assets are acquired, depreciated on the books, and never formally revalued to reflect what they'd actually sell for today.

The fix required rebuilding the equity and fixed-asset schedules from source documents and incorporating a formal property revaluation. The restated position supported a EGP 296M valuation — substantially above the book-value baseline the company would have entered negotiations from without the restatement.

This pattern — assets understated at historical cost, equity understated as a result, buyers knowing this and pricing accordingly — shows up repeatedly in Egyptian middle-market sell-side processes. The sellers often know their business is worth more than the books say but can't articulate why to a sophisticated buyer without the supporting documentation. The restatement is the documentation.

The lesson extends beyond Egypt: book value is a legal and accounting concept, not a market concept. In any sale process, the opening question is always "what do we actually own and what is it actually worth?" — not "what do the books say?" Starting from the books and working forward misses the point. Starting from the assets and building the case from there gets you to a defensible position that can survive a buyer's due diligence process.
