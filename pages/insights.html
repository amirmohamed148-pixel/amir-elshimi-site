Notes from the work itself.

Short, practitioner-level observations drawn from real engagements — not theory, patterns.

JUN 2026
Investment memos

Why most IC memos hide the downside case

The fastest way to lose a room is to only show them the number you want them to remember. Most investment committee memos I've reviewed — including early drafts of my own — lead with the base case or, worse, the upside scenario, and bury the conservative numbers in an appendix nobody opens.

On a greenfield catering investment I modeled recently, the scenario range ran from a 7-month payback in the best case to a meaningfully longer one in the conservative case. The temptation is always to lead with the headline IRR. The memo that actually earns trust leads with the case that's hardest to defend, then shows why the upside is plausible on top of it — not instead of it.

A committee that feels like it's being sold the best-case scenario stops trusting every other number in the document. One that sees the downside treated with the same rigor reads the rest of the memo differently.

JUN 2026
Due diligence

A number that can't be traced isn't a number yet

On a sell-side due diligence engagement for an industrial manufacturer, the pitch deck contained two different valuation figures across two documents, never reconciled. Neither was wrong on its own terms — they used different assumptions — but nobody had flagged that the two numbers disagreed.

This happens more often than it should, and it's rarely deliberate. Models get updated, decks don't. Assumptions change in a spreadsheet, but the slide built six weeks earlier never gets touched. The fix isn't more modeling sophistication — it's a basic discipline: every number in a client-facing document should trace back to a single source file, and someone should check that trace before the document goes out.

The earlier this check happens, the cheaper the fix. Catching it in due diligence is good. Catching it before the deck is built is better.

MAY 2026
Turnarounds

Diagnose before you prescribe

A Saudi trading company had lost more than half its equity value over seven years, and the working assumption inside the business was that weak consumer demand was the cause. Decomposing the financials line by line told a different story: gross margins and revenue were largely stable. The actual driver was G&A cost inflation, quietly compounding year after year.

The distinction matters because the wrong diagnosis leads to a remedy that makes things worse. A demand problem gets fixed with pricing and marketing. A cost problem gets fixed by cutting overhead. Apply the demand remedy to a cost problem and you erode margins further while the real issue keeps compounding.

Most turnaround conversations start with a confident diagnosis already in the room. The first job isn't to solve the stated problem — it's to check whether the stated problem is the real one.

MAY 2026
Cross-border practice

The structure changes by market. The discipline doesn't

Modeling a US hospitality acquisition after years of Egyptian and Gulf real estate work was a useful test of what actually transfers across markets. The structural conventions are genuinely different — a GP/LP waterfall with preferred-return tiers isn't how deals get structured in Cairo or Riyadh, and the regulatory and currency context changes everything around the model.

What didn't change was the underwriting discipline underneath it: stress-test the assumptions, model multiple exit scenarios, and never let the structure's complexity substitute for scrutiny of the underlying cash flows. The waterfall is just a different way of dividing a number. The work of getting that number right is the same work, in any currency.
